(Not recently, I hasten to add - my last attempt was in 2006)

These are strictly my opinions and have no basis in peer-reviewed studies (lol). I'm sure it's different for women, too, like everything else. I'm only talking about responsible adult men here.

First I want to this link which is one of the more sensible ones I've seen:

It's a bit feminine and gay though, as are most of them.
  • Three groups are particularly at risk: chronically ill (mentally/physically); veterans; separated dads.

    I imagine/hope the first group will be in contact with health professionals anyway who should be aware of the issues. There are support services for the other two but blokes don't reach out.
  • Don't bother to feel sorry for the suicidal "victim" himself - although he may be in pain, the real victims will be the bereaved (friends, family, loved ones, community in general) that he leaves behind. It's ok to be angry with him for considering such a selfish and cruel act. But don't guilt-trip him too much, it'll make him reluctant to talk to you.
  • DO talk to him, if you're worried. If you're not sure if he's actually suicidal, ask him. Don't beat around the bush: "Do you feel like killing yourself? Please don't."
  • Don't bother with arguments like "life will get better" and "it's a permanent solution to a temporary problem" or basically anything around the idea that he will somehow regret it. He knows he's not going to regret it; he's not going to be around to do the regretting.
  • Use humour to lighten things up. "Do the flip faggot!" The message is: it's okay to talk about this, I'm comfortable talking about this with you.
  • Ask him what he thinks will happen to him after he dies. If he thinks there's any kind of consciousness after a death by suicide, it might not be pleasant.
  • The most persuasive argument I have heard is a selfish one: "I don't want you to do this because I, personally, will miss you. I like having you around and it will suck if you're not here."
  • DON'T call the authorities (police, health, social services). These kinds of intervention will inevitably make life more difficult for him. That could easily make the suicide more likely. The intervention might stop him from doing it today but he'll probably just get around to it later. Meanwhile he has learned to keep it secret which just makes it worse for everyone else if/when it happens.

    Call support services for yourself if you want, if you're worried and want advice and support, but don't tell them who you're worried about.

    A note about this from the https://livethroughthis.org site above:
    NOTE: Many of these resources utilize restrictive interventions, like active rescues (wellness or welfare checks) involving law enforcement or emergency services. If this is a concern for you, you can ask if this is a possibility at any point in your conversation. Trans Lifeline does not implement restrictive interventions for suicidal people without express consent.
    So trannies get special treatment: "we won't call the cops on you". Everyone should be entitled to that respect.
  • My absolute all-time favourite strategy for dealing with a suicidal man: give him a job.

    This will take some effort from you. I truly believe this can work if you're determined to stop this guy from jumping.

    Put him to work. He's had too much time on his own thinking about shit that doesn't matter. He probably feels worthless. If you can find something useful for him to do it'll take his mind off his problems and he'll start to feel valuable.

    Simple, mindless, physical labour is good. Chop wood, stack shelves, sweep up garbage, do deliveries, whatever. He will need supervision and sincere feedback to keep him focused.

    He needs to keep busy doing something that other people find valuable.
